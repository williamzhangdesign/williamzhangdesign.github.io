Capital One: Product Selector

Reducing friction in our potential customer's card application journey by refreshing the Product Selector web component.

Our mission: Empower and improve our customer's lives by connecting them with the best credit card that fits their needs.

Overview

The Product Selector Component lives on the Credit Card Homepage (CCHP), which is the top of the marketing funnel for prospective Capital One card customers.


First launched in 2022, this component serves as a crucial tool to getting customers to start their application, or learn more about products.

We continued iterating on the component, serving potential customers to meet our evolving business goals.

Business Context

Oppportunity sizing

The CCHP is the highest traffic and most valuable revenue-generating page, with the most applications and new accounts booked.

Working with our data analyst and product manager, we identified the Product Selector as a major opportunity area to improve page performance, since it is the primary entrypoint for customers to begin their application.

With a 5% increase in new accounts booked, annual revenue would grow by $14 million dollars.

Old Product Selector from 2022.

Problems

With so many opportunities for failure in the card application journey, we narrowed down our problems to customers not being able to:
find credit cards that fit their needs (Product Discovery),
have enough information to proceed with applying (Lack of Product Knowledge),
which leads to difficulty in starting applications, preventing customers from becoming cardholders, reducing bookings and revenue.

Solving for Product Discovery

Problem: Customers can't find the product that fits their needs, leading to difficulty in starting their card applications.

Understanding the Problem

To solve for customers pain-points surrounding credit card discovery, we first looked at engagement data. In terms of customer interaction, the product selector's dropdown only had 5% engagement. This means potential customers:

1. are only looking at the three featured products (missing out on 30 other products that may be a better fit)

2. aren't being exposed to the 7 more specific product categories

Product Selector Dropdown, note 2 hidden categories that are revealed only by scrolling.

Rapid Competitive Analysis

Lacking competitive benchmarking, we delivered product a quick analysis of top competitors and their main credit card page to our product partners. Pulling primarily from banking and fintech, we found that competitors:

1. reduced the number of interactions 1 tap button/link instead of 2-tap dropdown

2. surfaced categories directly on the page, instead of hiding it behind a dropdown

This revealed deeper learnings- that our shared customer audience needed efficient interactions and visible card categories when they're browsing card products.

Redesign, Implementation, and Market Testing

After creating numerous prototypes in medium to high fidelity, I worked with product to select a solution that reduces the number of interactions and visible card categories, also leveraging our design system in order to save engineering bandwidth.

This "ghost-button" design was the final design put in the market for A/B testing. In the process of providing design support and QA during the implementation process, I noticed text-alignment problems (See "Apply Now" in the buttons above) with engineers, adressing these problems together. This was communicated to our enterprise design team, which was put on their backlog.

After a month, we learned that these variants underperformed compared to our original design and they were pulled from the market. This was a temporary setback, as we soon set out to test our 2nd hypothesis.

Solving for Lack of Product Knowledge

Understanding the Problem

With the second hypothesis, we set out to solve customers pain-points around having sufficient product information. In order to get customers through the application journey, before they can make a decision, we believed that potential customers need to know:

1. sufficient information about products they're interested in to make a decision (understanding of product terms)

2. where to go to retrieve this information (wayfinding/navigation)

Product Selector Dropdown, note 2 hidden categories that are revealed only by scrolling.

Redesign, Implementation, and Market Testing

After auditing the component this time around, we realized that entry-points into the application could be confusing. With a hard constraint of certain product terms, some of our products were not eligible for pre-approval (a soft hit to credit to view eligible card products). This gave us the real estate we needed to give our potential customers an entry-point to product pages with more specific information.

This "ghost-button" design was the final design put in the market for A/B testing. In the process of providing design support and QA during the implementation process, I fixed the "Apply Now" text-alignment problems with development in this release, and brought up text alignment problems to the enterprise design team to put on their backlog.

After a month, we learned that these variants underperformed compared to our original design and they were pulled from the market. This was a temporary setback, as we soon set out to test our 2nd hypothesis.
