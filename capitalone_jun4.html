Capital One: Product Selector

Reducing customer friction in the customer's card application journey by refreshing the Product Selector web component.

Our mission: to connect prospective Capital One customers to the best possible card product that fits their needs.

Overview

The Product Selector Component lives on the Credit Card Homepage (CCHP), which is the top of the marketing funnel for prospective Capital One card customers.


First launched in 2022, this component serves as a crucial tool in helping customers start their application or learn more about the credit cards we offer.

We continued iterating on the component, constantly learning and advocating for our customer as we simultaneously meet our evolving business goals.

Business Context

Oppportunity sizing

The CCHP is the highest traffic and revenue-generating page, with the most applications and new accounts booked.

Working with our data analyst and product manager, we identified the Product Selector as a major opportunity area to improve page performance, since it is the primary entrypoint for customers to begin their application.

With a 5% increase in new accounts booked, annual revenue would grow an estimated $14 million dollars.

Old Product Selector from 2022.

Customer Problems

With our product partners, we hypothesized that customers can't find credit cards that fit their needs (Unfound Product Options),
or that customers interested in a product don't have enough information to proceed with applying (Lack of Product Knowledge),

which leads to difficulty in starting applications, preventing customers from becoming cardholders, reducing bookings and revenue.

Solving for Unfound Product Options

Problem Framing

We started by solving for customers pain-points surrounding browsing and finding cards through the selector. In terms of interaction, the dropdown only had 5% engagement. This means 95% of potential customers:

1. are only looking at the three featured products (missing out on 30 other products that may be a better fit)
2. aren't being exposed to the 7 more specific product categories

Product Selector Dropdown, note 2 hidden categories that are revealed only by scrolling.

Rapid Competitive Analysis

Lacking competitive benchmarking, we approached product partners with a quick analysis of top competitors, specifically evaluating their main credit card page and ability to drive customers through their journey of exploration and information retrieval. Pulling primarily from banking, we found that the competitors:

1. reduced the number of interactions 1 tap button/link instead of 2-tap dropdown
2. surfaced categories directly on the page, instead of hiding it behind a dropdown

This revealed deeper learnings- that our competitor's customers are treated to more efficient interactions and visible card categories when they're browsing card products.

Design Audit

Before starting the redesign process, I called out issues specific to desktop:

1. alignment problems for the "Apply Now" text within CTA buttons, which was visually uneven and unprofessional
2. CTA horizontal alignment discreptancies across all 3 credit cards, which hinders the customer's ability to quickly scan and interact

After mocking up properly aligned elements, I socialized the CTA button text-alignment issue to the enterprise design team to put on their backlog to fix across the entire enterprise. In the meantime, I worked with our engineers to fix alignment issues internally for this test.


Redesign & Implementation

As for the redesign, I created numerous variants in medium to high fidelity using Figma, working with product to select a solution that reduces the number of interactions and visible card categories, also leveraging our design system in order to save engineering bandwidth.

This "ghost-button" tabbed design beat other similar options because of its ability to support mobile interactions and reduce additional scrolling. It was then chosen as the final design put in the market for A/B testing.

A/B Testing & Learnings

After a month, we learned that our redesign underperformed compared to our original design.

Even though this solution was unsuccessful, we learned that potential customers were very interested in exploring different credit card categories and products. However, this does not always translate into action in moving forward with the application.

This major learning set the stage, reinforcing the opportunity for us to solve for potential customer's clear lack of product knowledge and information. If we could equip them with this information that they seek, and empower them to take action, we could greatly increase the chance of winning for the business.

Solving for Lack of Product Knowledge

Problem Framing

This time, we were laser focused on solving potential customers pain-points around having sufficient product information. Before potential customers can make a decision, they need to know:

1. sufficient information about products they're interested in to make a decision (understanding of product terms)
2. where, and how to go to retrieve this information (wayfinding/navigation)

Design Audit

We first started by auditing the component, looking for previous design choices that could cause confusion for potential customers. We realized:

1. links to the product didn't properly signify the ability to go to a product's information page, preventing customers from exploring and learning more specific details (Improper signifiers)
2. credit card description text wasn't always useful, and didn't consistently inform a card's main use (Inconsistent copy)
3. certain credit cards not eligible for pre-approvalonly had an "Apply Now" CTA, and lacked a clear CTA entrypoint to help the customer get more information about a specific product (Lacking entrypoints)

Design audit annotations, also note successfully fixed visual problems which was pushed to our enterprise design system. Additional changes included correct consistency for CTAs, flipping colors for "See if I'm pre-approved" and "Apply Now" to correctly fit customer mental models.

Redesign & Partner Collaboration

We intended to use different UX treatments to improve the way we educate and guide customers. I made changes to information architecture, accessibility, content strategy, and standardized CTAs with different entry-points, creating multiple variants.

With a divergent mindset, these variants served as a good starting point. I then worked with product to converge, modify, and choose variants that given our tech resources, wouldn't overwhelm our awesome engineers, and would feasibly receive buy-in from business leadership. Testing (A/B/n=8) & Learnings

After a month, we learned that out of 8 variants, one variant with multiple interventions that included:

1. swapping QuicksilverOne for the more popular QuickersilverOne credit card (Marketing changes)
2. underlining the Product text-link to a specific product page to signify the ability to learn more (Proper signifiers)
3. introducing a "View Card Details" CTA button that took the customer to a specific product page if that product didn't have pre-approval (Clear entrypoints to gain crucial product information)

beat out 7 other variants with statistical signifiance, leading to a 4.7% lift in new accounts booked. Assuming the metrics were annualized, revenue would increase by a whopping $13.7m!

With constant incremental testing and changes, I'm happy to announce that this variant is still live today, and can be viewed at https://www.capitalone.com/credit-cards/

Takeaways

Cross Functional Relationships

Over the course of this work, I was constantly tailoring communication with product partners, empathizing for developers, and thinking about how we can involve separate design teams to improve the customer's experience.

During the design process, thoughtfully bringing product along in key moments, and being problem-oriented led to success for the customer, and the business.


Systems-level thinking

Though this case study deeply focuses on a single component with a lot of exposure, I constantly thought about entry-points to and from this page, as well as the purpose of the page- which we identified as getting customers to product-specific pages if they weren't ready to apply.

I also looked to our design system as a source of inspiration and truth, but also found ways to contribute and improve our design system. This created great relationships with enterprise design, and we created a fruitful feedback loop.


Navigating Ambiguity & Failure

Breaking down ambiguous problems early on was the biggest challenge I faced. The second, was being comfortable with failure, and learning how to trust that the iterative design process will bring us one step closer to a non-perfect solution that solves customer problems.

I know that this solution isn't the best one, and that one day the Product Selector will change again, or maybe it will be gone. But I am proud of the team that I worked with, the things we learned, and most importantly, the way we engaged and supported each other.
