Capital One: Product Selector

Reducing customer friction in the customer's card application journey by refreshing the Product Selector web component.

Our mission: to connect prospective Capital One customers to the best possible card product that fits their needs.

Overview

The Product Selector Component lives on the Credit Card Homepage (CCHP), which is the top of the marketing funnel for prospective Capital One card customers.


First launched in 2022, this component serves as a crucial tool in helping customers start their application or learn more about the credit cards we offer.

We continued iterating on the component, constantly learning and advocating for our customer as we simultaneously meet our evolving business goals.

Business Context

Oppportunity sizing

The CCHP is the highest traffic and most valuable revenue-generating page, with the most applications and new accounts booked.

Working with our data analyst and product manager, we identified the Product Selector as a major opportunity area to improve page performance, since it is the primary entrypoint for customers to begin their application.

With a 5% increase in new accounts booked, annual revenue would grow an estimated $14 million dollars.

Old Product Selector from 2022.

Customer Problems

With our product partners, we hypothesized that customers can't find credit cards that fit their needs (Unfound Product Options),
or that customers interested in a product don't have enough information to proceed with applying (Lack of Product Knowledge),

which leads to difficulty in starting applications, preventing customers from becoming cardholders, reducing bookings and revenue.

Solving for Unfound Product Options

Problem Framing

We started by solving for customers pain-points surrounding browsing and finding cards through the selector. In terms of interaction, the dropdown only had 5% engagement. This means 95% of potential customers:

1. are only looking at the three featured products (missing out on 30 other products that may be a better fit)

2. aren't being exposed to the 7 more specific product categories

Product Selector Dropdown, note 2 hidden categories that are revealed only by scrolling.

Rapid Competitive Analysis

Lacking competitive benchmarking, we approached product partners with a quick analysis of top competitors and their main credit card page (deck not shown due to NDA). Pulling primarily from banking and fintech, we found that competitors:

1. reduced the number of interactions 1 tap button/link instead of 2-tap dropdown

2. surfaced categories directly on the page, instead of hiding it behind a dropdown

This revealed deeper learnings- that our competitor's customers are treated to more efficient interactions and visible card categories when they're browsing card products.

Redesign & Implementation

Simultaneously during the design process, I called out issues specific to desktop:
1. alignment problems for the "Apply Now" text within CTA buttons, which was visually unprofessional
2. CTA horizontal alignment discreptancies across all 3 credit cards, which hinders the customer's ability to scan quickly

After mocking up properly aligned elements, I socialized the text within CTA buttons issue to the enterprise design team to put on their backlog to fix across the entire enterprise. In the meantime, I worked with our engineers to fix alignment issues internally for this test, supporting them during the implementation and QA process.

As for the actual redesign, creating numerous variants in medium to high fidelity, I worked with product to select a solution that reduces the number of interactions and visible card categories, also leveraging our design system in order to save engineering bandwidth. This "ghost-button" design beat other similar options because of its ability to support mobile interactions and reduce additional scrolling. It was then chosen as the final design put in the market for A/B testing.

A/B Testing & Learnings

After a month, we learned that our redesign underperformed compared to our original design.

Even though this solution was unsuccessful, we learned that potential customers were very interested in exploring different credit card categories and products. However, this does not always translate into action in moving forward with the application.

This major learning set the stage, reinforcing the opportunity for us to solve for potential customer's clear lack of product knowledge and information. If we could equip them with this information that they seek, and empower them to take action, we could greatly increase the chance of winning for the business.

Solving for Lack of Product Knowledge

Problem Framing

This time, we were laser focused on solving potential customers pain-points around having sufficient product information. Before potential customers can make a decision, they need to know:

1. sufficient information about products they're interested in to make a decision (understanding of product terms)

2. where, and how to go to retrieve this information (wayfinding/navigation)

Design Audit

We first started by auditing the component, looking for previous design choices that could cause confusion for potential customers. We realized:

1. links to the product didn't properly signify the ability to go to a product's information page, preventing customers from exploring and learning more specific details
2. credit card description text wasn't always useful
3. certain credit cards that were not eligible for pre-approval (a soft hit to credit to view eligible card products) only had an "Apply Now" CTA, and lacked a clear CTA entrypoint to help the customer get more information about a specific product

Redesign & Partner Collaboration

Before the redesign, the enterprise design team implemented changes that corrected consistency for CTAs, flipping colors for "See if I'm pre-approved" and "Apply Now" to correctly fit customer mental models.

We intended to test different treatments to improve the way we educate and guide customers. I made changes to information architecture, accessibility, content strategy, and standardized CTAs with different entry-points, creating multiple variants.

With a divergent mindset, these variants served as a starting point. I then worked with product to converge, modify, and choose variants that given our tech resources, wouldn't overwhelm our awesome engineers, and would feasibly receive buy-in from business leadership. Screenshots of the original variants I designed cannot be shown due to NDA, since the exact designs weren't implemented in market testing.

A/B/n=8 Testing & Learnings

After a month, we learned that one variant, with multiple interventions that included:

1. swapping QuicksilverOne for the more popular QuickersilverOne credit card (Marketing changes)
2. underlining the Product text-link to a specific product page to signify the ability to learn more (Proper signifiers)
3. introducing a "View Card Details" CTA button that took the customer to a specific product page if that product didn't have pre-approval (Clear entrypoints to gain crucial product information)

beat out 7 other variants, leading to a 4.7% lift in new accounts booked. Assuming the design was implemented and metrics were annualized, revenue would increase by a whopping $13.7m!
